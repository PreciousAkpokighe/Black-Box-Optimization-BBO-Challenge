# Week 9 Reflection – Emergent Phenomena and Scaling Laws in BBO Capstone

By week 9 of my BBO project I had 18 data points and the optimisation was starting to show some more obvious scaling laws. We’ve seen this in previous rounds where minor variations in input have led to big differences in performance. For Function 5 in particular, the outputs were significantly higher when using a region that had been successful in the past. But the more data I added I began to see some functions had diminishing returns. Incremental change was often purchased with marginal gains in the places already passed through. This demonstrated the performance does not scale with the number of samples, a scaling behavior typical for machine learning systems.

Emergent behavior changed my planning, my thinking, too. Much better results (in particular for Function 5) are obtained in a high performance region than in totally new regions.  Some functions had hidden non-linearity, so small improvements could lead to surprisingly large improvements. I have therefore developed a hybrid optimisation approach (balancing the exploitation of promising regions with the risk of exploration in more uncertain regions).  I chose bayesian optimisation over random search. It employs Gaussian Process modelling to estimate uncertainty and smartly select the next query.

Now the trade-off between cost, robustness and performance was becoming clearer. Aggressive exploration increases the computational cost and wastes queries on low quality regions Risk of overexploitation of local optima. So my new approach was robustness, where I took successful patterns from previous weeks, and did moderate exploration in areas of high uncertainty.  The performance trends in the historical data were stable. Therefore, the query structures of functions 1 and 5 were also stable. Functions 6-8, less predictable in behavior, continued to venture into larger territories.

Another important lesson was the tension between predictable optimization and the possibility of sudden emergent gains. Strong, repeatable, predictable optimization.    But black-box systems can also conceal opportunities that can only be uncovered through patient experimentation. I handled this ambiguity in the Week 9 notebook with adaptive search radii and emergence-aware scoring. Functions with large historical gains had less search boundaries for exploitation .  Functions with weak or unstable gains had larger search regions to allow them to find new patterns.
