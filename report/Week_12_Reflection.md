# 1. I have changed my optimisation approach from a wide, exploratory sampling to a more structured, data-driven approach. In the first rounds I used evenly distributed query points in order to get a feeling of the behaviour of each unknown function. As the number of observations grew, I added surrogate modelling, Bayesian-inspired optimisation, clustering techniques and PCA-based dimensionality reduction to help direct query selection. It is now a more systematic process, rather than a random exploration, as each new query is informed by historical performance, local neighbourhood analysis and variance patterns. This has led to improved consistency, particularly for Functions 5, 7 and 8, where positive trends have been seen over multiple rounds.

2. Functions 5, 7 and 8 appear to be the largest sources of variation because these dimensions showed the largest change in output when inputs were varied. PCA-like analysis indicates that a small fraction of input directions are responsible for a disproportionate fraction of performance improvements, with the other directions providing redundancy or noise. Function 5 was found to be particularly sensitive to exploitation around high performing regions and Functions 7 and 8 responded positively to controlled local refinement. In contrast, Functions 3, 4 and 6 continued to exhibit negative or unstable behaviour implying that the underlying performance drivers for these functions may be more complex or located in previously unexplored regions of the search space.

3. I focus on exploration in areas with high uncertainty and where the historical results exhibit erratic performance. For functions with stable positive trends, I cut down on unnecessary exploration and focus on local refinement around the best-performing regions. This is similar to PCA in the sense that one down-weights the uninformative dimensions and preserves the components that explain the most of the variance. For example, Function 5 has continually profited from exploitation of known strong regions, whereas Functions 3, 4 and 6 require continued exploration, as indicated by their negative outputs, that important patterns have not yet been fully identified.

4. This round serves as a reminder of the importance of balancing exploration and exploitation . For the final round, I plan to put more effort into exploiting the known high-performing areas for Functions 5,7, and 8 and retain a smaller but intentional exploration budget for Functions 3,4, and 6. The PCA-based approach detects the most contributing dimensions to the performance and allows to focus the resources to the most informative parts of the search space. This should improve efficiency while still allowing the possibility of discovering better performing regions.

5. Since PCA focuses on identifying the factors that explain most of the variation in performance, it provides a useful framework for interpreting BBO results. Instead of considering all the input dimensions as equally important, PCA encourages to focus on the most informative patterns and to drop redundant information. In this project this view was used to find out which functions were answering to optimisation efforts in a reliable way and which ones were adding noise or uncertainty. By focusing on the dimensions that cause significant changes to the output, the optimization process becomes more efficient, more interpretable and better aligned with the goal of finding high-performing solutions in a complex black-box environment.
